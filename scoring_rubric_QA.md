# Video Interview Scoring Rubric — QA Specialist

## Instructions
Score each criterion **1 to 4**. A score of 1 or 4 is expected whenever the evidence clearly supports it — do not default to 2 or 3. Record the total and the per-criterion scores in the candidate's notes.

---

## Scoring Rubric

### 1. Role Fit and Relevant Experience (gating criterion)

*What it measures:* Does their actual background meet the core bar for the role? Real experience reviewing content or materials for accuracy — not just proofreading.

| Score | Description | Behavioral Signals |
|:---:|:---|:---|
| **4** | Directly on point; could contribute almost immediately. | **Score 4 if ANY of the following is true:** (a) Owns or has owned a formal content QA role as primary reviewer of educational or digital materials for accuracy. (b) Holds a substantive educational content leadership role (content director, curriculum coordinator, department lead) PLUS has deep educational domain expertise (years of teaching, curriculum writing, or instructional design) PLUS describes catching a specific educational accuracy error in their own work — such as identifying that assessments did not align with taught content, or that a standard was mislabeled in a curriculum product. **Two-part test:** Could this candidate start contributing to content accuracy review immediately? AND Do they describe specific content accuracy errors they personally caught? If both answers are YES, score 4. A formal "QA" job title is NOT required. |
| **3** | Clear, relevant experience that maps to the role. | Has reviewed educational or digital materials for accuracy, consistency, or functional correctness. Can describe a specific error they caught. Understands that QA differs from copy-editing. **OR** is an experienced educator (classroom teacher, curriculum specialist) who has systematically reviewed educational materials for accuracy, curriculum alignment, or content quality — even if not in a dedicated QA role. The signal is whether they have caught specific content accuracy errors in educational materials, not whether they held a QA job title. |
| **2** | Adjacent experience only; would need significant ramp. | Has done proofreading or editing for grammar/spelling but no systematic QA process. Experience is with informal or creative content, not structured educational or digital product materials. **Note: seniority level (intern, junior, senior) is NOT the discriminator between 2 and 3. The discriminator is whether the work was a systematic accuracy review of structured content — not just grammar edits. A candidate who was an unpaid intern running real QA checks on a digital learning platform qualifies for a 3.** |
| **1** | No relevant experience to the core bar. | Background is entirely unrelated — no example of reviewing materials for content accuracy. Cannot name a single instance of catching a content error (factual, alignment, or functional) in an educational or learning material. Note: describing formatting checks (fonts, spacing, visual consistency) is NOT equivalent to content accuracy review and does not qualify for a 1-point bump. |

### 2. Domain Judgment

*What it measures:* Shows instinct for spotting content and accuracy errors — understands that different error types have different severity.

| Score | Description | Behavioral Signals |
|:---:|:---|:---|
| **4** | Deep, nuanced understanding; gives concrete examples. | **Score 4 if BOTH of the following are true:** (1) The candidate names multiple distinct error types — including at least one curriculum-level or content-accuracy category (e.g., standards alignment, assessment-content alignment, factual inaccuracy) — not just grammar and spelling. (2) The candidate gives a concrete, personal example of finding one of those errors in their actual work. **Explicit severity-ranking language is NOT required for Score 4.** The act of personally catching a curriculum-integrity error (e.g., "I noticed the assessments didn't align with what we were actually teaching") IS the evidence of severity awareness — the candidate has demonstrated they distinguish high-stakes errors from surface-level ones through what they actually caught, not through how they describe their philosophy. |
| **3** | Solid, specific understanding. | Articulates at least two distinct error categories and can explain why each matters for the learner or user. Goes beyond "typos and grammar." At least one category named should be a curriculum-level or content-accuracy concern (e.g., standards alignment, assessment alignment, grade-level appropriateness, factual accuracy) — not just surface presentation errors. |
| **2** | General awareness, few specifics. | Mentions accuracy and quality but cannot differentiate error types or explain why some errors are more serious than others. Describes catching obvious mistakes but shows no systematic thinking about less visible errors. |
| **1** | Little understanding shown. | Cannot name a single error category beyond typos. Treats QA as proofreading. No awareness of functional, factual, or accessibility error types. |

---

### 3. Process and Methodology

*What it measures:* Structured, systematic approach to the task they describe.

| Score | Description | Behavioral Signals |
|:---:|:---|:---|
| **4** | Rigorous and thoughtful about edge cases. | Describes a documented, multi-pass process with explicit steps for different error categories. Addresses edge cases: what to do when unsure, how to decide whether a fix is an editor fix or a developer ticket. Process is designed to scale. **No formal tool (checklist software, tracking system) or senior QA vocabulary is required for a 4 — the signal is whether the candidate demonstrates judgment about when to escalate vs. self-resolve, and can articulate steps another person could replicate.** |
| **3** | Clear, repeatable approach. | Describes a repeatable review sequence with multiple named steps. Uses or describes a checklist or structured approach. Can explain how they ensure consistency across multiple items. *Example that earns a 3: "I do a layout pass, then proofread all text, then test every interaction, then a final walkthrough against my notes" — a documented multi-step sequence, regardless of how casually phrased.* **Another example that earns a 3: "First I look at it in its entirety, then I go through it with my checklist, then I make sure it's consistent throughout the unit." This is a three-stage process — (1) holistic overview, (2) structured checklist-based item review, (3) cross-unit consistency check — and qualifies for Score 3 even when described informally. The steps do not need to be formally named; describing what you do first, next, and last is sufficient.** A candidate who adds a time-boxed troubleshooting rule AND an explicit escalation judgment call (e.g., "I asked whether to put in a ticket or change it myself") alongside their review sequence is describing a 4, not a 3. |
| **2** | Some structure, but gaps remain. | Has a loose sequence (read once, fix obvious things) but no documented checklist, consistent framework, or method to ensure nothing is missed. |
| **1** | No clear method. | Reviews "as they go" or "reads through once." Cannot describe what they check first, second, or last. No evidence of a systematic approach. |

---

### 4. Communication

*What it measures:* Clear, concise, organized, easy to follow. Note: a narrative or story-format Q1 answer (describing a past experience) is not inherently "roundabout" — setting context before the key signal emerges is normal. The question is whether the listener can follow the through-line without getting lost. Repetitive phrasing that restates the same point without advancing it, or an answer that circles back without landing on a concrete example, IS "roundabout" regardless of format. Spoken filler words ("you know," "so yeah," "things like that," "I say we," "um") are artifacts of spoken delivery — they do **NOT** move a score down under any circumstances. Score 3 if the substance is followable and the listener can track each answer's through-line, even when the delivery is casual and contains filler phrases. **Example of a score-3 response with filler words:** "So, you know, the first thing I do is, um, look at everything in its entirety... I have checklists, I have a checklist for, you know, almost everything — things like that..." — if all questions are substantively answered and the content is trackable, this is a 3 regardless of verbal fillers.

| Score | Description | Behavioral Signals |
|:---:|:---|:---|
| **4** | Crisp, confident, and persuasive. | Uses specific, precise language. Structures each answer so a listener can follow without effort. Demonstrates the communication quality needed to write unambiguous bug tickets or revision notes for a development team. |
| **3** | Clear and well organized. | Answers each question directly. Easy to follow without re-listening. No significant tangents. **For the bug-escalation question (Q3), a Score 3 is appropriate when the candidate articulates a clear decision principle — even informally stated — such as "I fix what I can myself, and if it's something I can't do on my own I'd call in a developer." This is a direct, followable answer to Q3. Do NOT score Q3 as a 2 merely because it is brief or lacks elaborated mechanics; Score 2 for Q3 requires that a listener genuinely cannot determine what the candidate would do.** |
| **2** | Understandable with some effort. | Answers the question but takes a roundabout path. Some sentences are unclear or run on. Requires significant inference to extract meaning — the listener cannot readily determine what the candidate would actually do. |
| **1** | Hard to follow or rambling. | Loses the thread mid-answer, contradicts themselves, or cannot complete a thought. Would struggle to write a clear, actionable bug ticket. |

---

### 5. Instruction-Following and Professionalism

*What it measures:* Answered all three questions, on time, professional delivery. Note: this criterion measures question coverage and example quality — not structural organisation. If two answers blend together in delivery but both questions are substantively addressed with concrete examples, score based on coverage. Organisational clarity belongs under Communication, not here.

| Score | Description | Behavioral Signals |
|:---:|:---|:---|
| **4** | Exceeded expectations; polished and prepared. | Answered all three questions with depth and specificity. Came prepared with a concrete example for each question. Closing is professional and on-point. |
| **3** | Met all expectations. | Answered all three questions completely. Professional tone. No meaningful gaps in content. |
| **2** | Met most expectations. | Addressed all questions but one answer is thin, off-topic, or vague. |
| **1** | Missed questions or late. | Skipped or clearly missed one or more of the three questions. Submission is informal or incoherent. |

---

## Scoring Rules

**Maximum Score:** 20 points

* **Advance to interview:** Total of 14 or higher, AND Criterion 1 (Role Fit) scored 3 or higher.
* **Hold or borderline:** Total of 11 to 13. Discuss before deciding.
* **Decline:** Total of 10 or lower.
* **Automatic decline (hard fail):** Regardless of total score, automatically decline if the candidate:
    * Did not answer all three questions.
    * Has no experience meeting the role's core bar. **For this role, the core bar requires EITHER (a) experience reviewing educational, instructional, or structured learning materials for content accuracy — not merely visual formatting (fonts, spacing, bolding) — in any professional context, OR (b) substantive background in educational content creation, teaching, or curriculum development. A candidate whose only review experience is checking non-educational documents for visual formatting consistency, with no educational content background whatsoever, does NOT meet the core bar.**
    * Cannot communicate clearly enough to follow.
